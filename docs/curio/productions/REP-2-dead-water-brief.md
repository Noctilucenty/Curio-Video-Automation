## 1. Source packet

### Primary / authoritative sources

1. **Nansen, F. — *Farthest North* (1897).** Original account. August 1893,
   the *Fram*, near the Nordenskiöld Archipelago / Taymyr Peninsula, north of
   Siberia. Direct quote: *"When caught in dead water Fram appeared to be held
   back, as if by some mysterious force, and she did not always answer the
   helm."* Speed: the Fram normally made **6–7 knots**; caught in dead water
   she was **unable to make 1.5 knots** — roughly **a fifth of normal speed**.
   - via [Wikipedia: Dead water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dead_water)
   - corroborated: [Springer — "Calculating FRAM's Dead Water"](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-319-71934-4_6)

2. **Ekman, V. W. — *On Dead Water* (doctoral work, 1904).** At the request of
   Vilhelm Bjerknes, Ekman reproduced the phenomenon in laboratory tanks and
   demonstrated the mechanism: **internal waves generated at the interface
   between a fresh/brackish surface layer and denser salt water below**. A
   ship whose draught sits near that interface expends its propulsion energy
   creating and maintaining these subsurface waves instead of moving forward.
   The surface can remain completely calm.
   - via [Wikipedia: Dead water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dead_water)

3. **Fourdrinoy et al. — PNAS 2020, "The dual nature of the dead-water
   phenomenology: Nansen versus Ekman wave-making drags."** Modern
   confirmation and refinement (CNRS / Université de Poitiers): two drag
   regimes — **Nansen drag** (constant abnormal slowness) and **Ekman drag**
   (speed oscillations, shown to be a transient from the ship's initial
   acceleration); internal waves act "like an undulating conveyor belt" under
   the hull. Dead water "is seen in all seas and oceans where waters of
   different densities mix" — i.e. the phenomenon is current, not historical.
   - [PNAS 10.1073/pnas.1922584117](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1922584117)
   - [CNRS press release](https://www.cnrs.fr/en/press/behind-dead-water-phenomenon)
